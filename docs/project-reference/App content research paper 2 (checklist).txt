Personal Cybersecurity Health Check: A Comprehensive Checklist
In today’s digital world, keeping your personal data safe is essential. **Millions of people fall victim to identity theft and online scams each year, losing billions of dollars in the process 1**, but the good news is that following a few best practices can dramatically reduce your risk. This guide provides a step-by-step cybersecurity health check for non-technical users, based on reputable sources (government agencies, independent security organizations, and experts). Each section is a checklist item with brief explanations and tips. By completing this personal cybersecurity checklist, you can harden your defenses against hackers, scammers, and data thieves.



1. Password Hygiene
Why: Weak or reused passwords make it easy for hackers to break into your accounts. Strengthening your passwords is one of the most effective steps to improve security 2.
    • Use Strong Passphrases: Create passwords that are long, at least 12–16 characters (longer is better) and hard to guess 3. A great approach is to use a passphrase – a series of 4 or more random words or a sentence that you can remember, but others can’t easily guess (e.g. “crystal onion clay pretzel” as a single passphrase) 4. Longer passphrases are both more secure and easier to recall than short complex passwords 4. Avoid personal information or common words (no pet names or birthdays) to thwart guessing 3.
    • Make Every Password Unique: Never reuse passwords across different accounts 2. If you reuse the same password and one account is breached, criminals will try that password on your other accounts 2. Ensure each account (email, banking, social media, etc.) has its own unique password so a compromise of one won’t lead to a domino effect.
    • Use a Password Manager: It’s difficult to remember dozens of long, unique passwords. Consider using a reputable password manager program to securely generate and store your passwords for you 3 2. Password managers keep your credentials encrypted and can auto-fill them, so you only have to remember one master password. This allows you to use strong, random passwords everywhere without memorization 3. Even web browsers often have built-in password managers that can be used (just make sure they’re locked with a strong master password).
    • Change Passwords Periodically (Especially if Compromised): While the emphasis is on length and uniqueness, it’s also wise to update your passwords from time to time. Some experts recommend changing passwords every 90 days or at least once a year as a precaution 2. At minimum, change passwords immediately if you suspect an account has been breached or if a service you use reports a data breach. Regular rotation can limit the damage if someone did obtain your old password 2. (Note: If you use a password manager with very strong unique passwords, frequent changes are less critical, but important accounts like banking or email might still be changed annually for good measure.)
    • Bonus – Use Passphrases Where Possible: If a site allows long passwords but not spaces, you can run several words together with punctuation or numbers in between. For example, “Battery-Horse-Staple-Correct?” (just a random phrase) is very long and strong. Some systems may require a mix of uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and symbols – you can still incorporate those into a passphrase naturally (e.g., “1Banana+1Pineapple” as suggested by security experts 5). Length plus unpredictability is key.
(Checklist: Long, random, and unique passwords ✓ Use a password manager ✓ No password reuse ✓ Periodically update passwords ✓) 3 22. Device and Software Updates
Why: Keeping your devices and software up to date patches critical security vulnerabilities. Outdated software is a common way for attackers to gain unauthorized access.
    • Turn On Automatic Updates: Enable automatic system and app updates on all your devices (computers, phones, tablets) so that security patches install as soon as they’re available 6. Software updates often fix “bugs” and security holes that hackers can exploit 4. Many devices will alert you to updates – don’t click “Remind me later.” Instead, install the update promptly 6. Keeping your operating systems (Windows, macOS, iOS, Android), web browsers, and applications updated is one of the easiest ways to stay secure 6.
    • Install Updates Promptly: If automatic updates aren’t enabled or available, check for updates regularly and apply them. This is especially important for web browsers and any software that connects to the internet 6. Don’t delay critical updates – cybercriminals move quickly to take advantage of known flaws in unpatched software 6 4. As one tip puts it: “Malicious online criminals won’t wait, so we shouldn’t either!” 6
    • Upgrade Unsupported Devices/Apps: If you have very old devices or software that no longer receive updates (for example, an ancient phone or an OS like Windows 7 which has reached end-of-support), recognize that they can’t be fully secured 4. Consider upgrading to a supported version or newer device that gets regular security updates 4. Using obsolete, unpatchable software leaves you vulnerable to known exploits.
    • Keep Antivirus and Security Tools Updated: Make sure you have up-to-date security software on your computer, such as antivirus/anti-malware programs, and that it updates its virus definitions regularly 5. Many operating systems come with built-in security (Windows Defender, etc.) – ensure those are enabled and updated too. Security software can detect and block many threats, but it’s only effective if kept current with the latest threat signatures 5.
    • Apply Firmware Updates: Remember to update the firmware on things like your Wi-Fi router, smart devices, and other connected gadgets when updates are available (more on router security below). These devices can also have vulnerabilities, and updates keep them secure.
    • Bonus – Reboot Occasionally: A simple habit like restarting your computer or phone periodically can help apply updates and even clear certain malware from memory 7. For example, weekly reboots ensure updates finalize and can disrupt any malicious code that only lives in memory 7.
(Checklist: Automatic updates on ✓ All software up-to-date ✓ Security software enabled ✓ Old unsupported tech replaced ✓) 6 43. Network Security (Wi-Fi & Home Internet)
Why: Your home Wi-Fi router is the gateway to all your connected devices. If it’s not secured, attackers could snoop on your traffic or break into your network. Taking a few steps to secure your Wi-Fi and being careful on networks keeps you safe online.
    • Secure Your Wi-Fi with Encryption: Make sure your wireless network is using WPA2 or WPA3 encryption (the security setting in your router) with a strong Wi-Fi password 7. WPA3 is the newest, most secure standard; WPA2 is acceptable if some devices don’t support WPA3. Avoid the outdated and easily-broken WEP or leaving your Wi-Fi “open” (no password) – that puts your network at high risk. Use a strong passphrase (ideally 12+ characters, mixed-case letters, numbers, symbols) for your Wi-Fi network key 7. This prevents outsiders from connecting or cracking your Wi-Fi. Modern routers often generate a strong default passphrase – if yours is default and printed on the router, consider changing it to one only you know.
    • Change Default Router Admin Credentials: The router’s admin username/password (used to configure settings) should be changed from the factory default 1. Default credentials (like “admin/admin”) are well-known and can allow someone to take over your router remotely. Set a strong unique password for the router’s admin login as well 1. This is different from the Wi-Fi password – it’s for the router’s settings panel.
    • Keep Your Router Firmware Updated: Periodically log in to your router’s admin interface to check for firmware updates, or enable automatic firmware updates if available. Router manufacturers release updates to fix security issues. An outdated router is a vulnerable router 7. If your router is provided by your ISP, they may push updates automatically – but if it’s your own device, you might need to check manually on the router’s web interface for a “firmware update” option.
    • Enable the Router Firewall: Most routers include a basic firewall/NAT that blocks unsolicited inbound connections. Ensure this is turned on (usually it is by default) 7. A router firewall helps prevent external attackers from directly reaching devices on your network.
    • Use a Guest Network for Visitors and IoT: If your router supports multiple wireless networks (SSID), consider setting up a separate guest Wi-Fi network for guests or less-secure devices 7 1. This way you can give friends Wi-Fi access without exposing your personal devices or data. Also, if you have smart home/IoT devices (cameras, smart TVs, etc.), you might isolate them on the guest network so they can’t directly access your primary devices 7. Segmentation like this adds security – if a guest or IoT device is compromised, it won’t have access to your laptops/phones on the main network.
    • Turn Off Unused Features: For example, disable remote administration of your router (so you can only log in to it from within your home network, not the internet) 7, unless you specifically need that. Also consider turning off WPS (Wi-Fi Protected Setup) if your router has it, as that feature can be exploited on some routers. These steps close potential loopholes an attacker could use from outside.
    • Be Cautious on Public Wi-Fi: When you’re away from home, be careful using public Wi-Fi networks (like those in cafes, hotels, or airports). Public Wi-Fi may be insecure – attackers on the same network might spy on your traffic. If you must use public Wi-Fi, avoid accessing sensitive accounts (banking, email) unless you use a VPN (Virtual Private Network) to encrypt your connection 1. A safer alternative is to use your smartphone’s cellular data or a personal hotspot for important transactions 1. Always ensure any website you log into over public Wi-Fi is using HTTPS (look for the padlock icon) so that your data is encrypted in transit. And never install updates or software while on untrusted networks; wait until you’re on a secure network.
    • Secure Your Network Equipment: Treat your modem/router and any network extenders as critical equipment. Physically secure them (so unauthorized folks can’t hit the reset button and revert to default settings). Also, periodically reboot your router (some experts suggest weekly) to clear out any potential memory-resident malware 7.
(Checklist: WPA2/3 encryption ✓ Strong Wi-Fi password ✓ Change default router password ✓ Router firewall on ✓ Guest network for others ✓ Caution on public Wi-Fi ✓) 7 14. Data Backups
Why: Backing up your data ensures that even if your device is lost, stolen, or infected with ransomware, you won’t lose your important files. It’s a safety net that every user should have as part of good cyber hygiene.
    • Back Up Your Important Data Regularly: Make regular backups of all critical files – such as documents, photos, contacts, and anything you don’t want to lose 8. A backup means keeping a copy of your data in a separate location from your computer/phone. You can back up to an external hard drive or USB flash drive, and/or use a cloud backup service. How often to back up depends on how often your data changes – many people aim for weekly or monthly backups, but if you create important new files daily, consider backing up daily or enabling continuous cloud backup. The key is to do it consistently so your backup is up-to-date 4.
    • Use the 3-2-1 Rule (multiple backup copies): A good practice is the “3-2-1” rule: keep 3 copies of your data (the original plus two backups), on 2 different media (for example, one in cloud storage and one on an external drive), with 1 copy stored off-site (or at least offline) for safety. This way, your data is resilient even if one backup fails or is destroyed. For example, you might use an external drive at home and also back up important files to a reputable cloud service – even if one is compromised, the other survives.
    • Keep a Backup Offline (Protect from Ransomware): Disconnect and safely store external backup drives when not in use 7. This is important because if ransomware infects your computer, it can encrypt any drive currently connected, including backup drives. By keeping one backup offline (unplugged) except when updating it, you ensure malware can’t reach it 7. Similarly, cloud backups that retain previous versions of files can help recover from ransomware encryption.
    • Back Up Mobile Device Data: Don’t forget your phone and tablet – ensure you back up your mobile devices too 8. For smartphones, you can use cloud backup solutions (like iCloud backup for iPhones or Google Backup for Android) or back up to a computer. This will save your contacts, photos, and app data so you can recover them if your phone is lost or wiped.
    • Verify Your Backups: A backup is only useful if it works when you need it. Periodically test your backups by restoring a file or two to make sure you know how to do it and that the files aren’t corrupt 4. Also check that new files are indeed being added to your backup on schedule. This gives you confidence that your recovery process will succeed in an emergency.
    • Secure Your Backups: Protect backup drives or accounts just like you protect your primary data. Keep external drives in a safe place (and consider encrypting them, so if someone finds the drive they can’t read your files). For cloud backups, use a strong unique password and enable MFA if available, since that data is also sensitive.
(Checklist: Files backed up to external drive/cloud ✓ Backup schedule set (e.g. weekly) ✓ One backup copy stored offline ✓ Backup of phone data ✓ Backup integrity tested ✓) 8 75. Privacy Settings on Apps and Browsers
Why: Many apps and websites collect more information than you might realize. By reviewing privacy settings, you can limit data sharing and exposure of your personal information. This improves your privacy and can reduce how much of your data is out there to be abused.
    • Tighten Privacy Settings on Social Media and Online Accounts: Go through the privacy settings of your social media accounts (Facebook, Instagram, Twitter/X, LinkedIn, etc.) and ensure they are configured to limit who can see your information 9. For example, set your posts and profile details to “Friends only” or private, rather than public. This helps manage your “digital footprint” so strangers can’t easily harvest your personal details 9. Most major platforms offer a privacy checkup tool or settings page – use those to disable public visibility of things like your friend list, posts, photos, and profile information. The U.K. National Cyber Security Centre specifically advises using these privacy controls so your personal info “remains inaccessible to anyone but you” 9.
    • Limit App Permissions: On your smartphone and computer, review the permissions you have granted to apps. Many apps (especially free ones) request access to data they don’t actually need. For each app, ask: does this app really need access to my location, camera, microphone, contacts, photos, etc.? Deny or revoke permissions that are not necessary 1. Both iOS and Android let you manage app permissions in Settings. Only give apps the permissions absolutely required for their function 1. For example, a flashlight app doesn’t need your contacts list, and a game probably doesn’t need your GPS location. By minimizing granted permissions, you reduce the risk of apps mishandling your private data.
    • Be Selective with Apps and Browser Extensions: Only install apps and browser extensions from reputable sources. Stick to official app stores (Google Play™ Store for Android, Apple’s App Store for iOS) and well-known publishers 1. Third-party app stores or random downloads can contain malicious apps that spy on you 1. Similarly, for browser extensions, choose those from trusted developers and remove extensions you no longer use. Each extension often can read your browsing data, so fewer is safer—install only what you truly need and has good reviews.
    • Configure Web Browser Privacy Settings: Web browsers have settings to enhance your privacy. Block third-party cookies in your browser settings to prevent advertisers and trackers from following you around the web 10. Third-party cookies collect your browsing habits and can pose privacy risks 10. Most browsers allow blocking these while still allowing first-party cookies (which are needed for website logins). Also consider turning off ad personalization or “interest-based ads” in the browser or Google account settings 10 – this limits the data shared for advertising. Regularly clear your cookies and cache, especially on shared computers, to remove stored data 10.
    • Restrict Website Permissions: Browsers will ask for permission if a site wants to use sensitive features like your location, camera, microphone, or send notifications. Grant these sparingly 10. If a website requests your location or camera and it’s not essential, click “Block.” Only allow such access for sites that absolutely need it (like video conferencing sites for camera/mic, or maps for location) 10. You can always manually enter location if needed rather than giving continuous access. Also, be cautious with browser notification requests – if allowed, a malicious site could spam you with fake notifications 10. It’s often safest to deny notification requests except from services you really trust.
    • Use Do Not Track and Privacy Tools: Enabling the “Do Not Track” setting in your browser can signal to websites that you don’t want to be tracked (though not all will honor it). Utilizing privacy-focused browser extensions or settings can also help; for example, some people use tracker-blocking extensions or DNS-based blockers. Just be mindful to choose widely trusted tools. Additionally, browse in Incognito/Private mode when on public or shared computers – it prevents the browser from saving history or cookies after your session (though it’s not a substitute for other privacy practices).
    • Review Privacy Policies (when realistic): When signing up for new apps or services, take a quick look at what data they collect. At least skim for red flags like “we share your data with third parties…”. Opt out of optional data collection when you have the choice. It’s also a good idea to periodically review your Google account or Apple ID privacy dashboard to see what data is collected and adjust settings (like turning off ad personalization, reviewing activity logs, etc.).
(Checklist: Social media accounts set to private/friends-only ✓ Unneeded app permissions revoked ✓ Third-party cookies blocked ✓ Untrusted apps/extensions removed ✓ Location/camera access only given when required ✓) 1 106. Recognizing Phishing and Scams
Why: Phishing is one of the most common ways people get hacked – you might be tricked into giving away your credentials or installing malware. Learning to spot scams before you fall for them is crucial for your cybersecurity.
    • Be Skeptical of Unsolicited Messages: Think twice when you get an unexpected email, text, or direct message, especially if it’s asking for personal information or urging you to click a link. Phishing messages often pretend to be from a trusted source (your bank, a popular company, or even someone you know) and create a false sense of urgency or importance 8. For example, a scam email might claim “Your account is about to be suspended, click here to verify now!” or offer something enticing like a prize or refund. Train yourself to spot red flags such as generic greetings (“Dear Customer” instead of your name), spelling or grammar mistakes, or sender addresses that don’t match the official organization (e.g., a bank email coming from gmail.com). Remember that legitimate companies rarely ask you to confirm sensitive info via email or text out of the blue 8.
    • Know the Common Phishing Tactics: Scammers often use psychological tricks like authority, urgency, emotion, scarcity, and current events to manipulate you 4. For example:
        ◦ Authority: “This is the IRS; you owe taxes…” or “Your boss needs this information now.”
        ◦ Urgency: “Act now or your account will be locked!” creating panic so you don’t think carefully 4.
        ◦ Emotion: making you fearful, curious, or excited (e.g., “You’ve won a lottery!” or “Your account was compromised!”).
        ◦ Scarcity: “Only the first 100 responders get this reward.”
        ◦ Current events: using news (like COVID-19 or natural disasters) as bait (“Donate here to relief fund…”).
Being aware of these tells can help you pause and question suspicious communications
4.
    • Don’t Click Links or Attachments from Strangers (or Unexpected Sources): As a rule, never click a link or open an attachment in an email or message that you weren’t expecting 1. Phishing emails can direct you to fake websites that look legitimate but steal your login details, or attachments might contain malware. Even if the message looks like it’s from a company you use, it’s safer not to click links in the email. Instead, navigate to the company’s official website by typing the address yourself or using a bookmark, or call their official customer service number if there’s an issue. Verify before you click. For attachments, unless you are 100% sure of the source, it’s best to delete or ignore unsolicited files – especially if they are .zip, .exe, or Office documents asking you to enable macros.
    • Verify Requests via Official Channels: If an email or call says it’s urgent to update your account or asks for personal data, don’t trust the contact info or links they provide. Contact the company through a known good method: for example, call the number on the back of your credit card or go to the official website by yourself 4. If the message was fraudulent, reaching out independently will reveal that (“No, we didn’t send that email”). Legitimate institutions won’t mind if you independently confirm their requests – they will understand your caution. No bank or government agency will ever email/call and just ask for your password, Social Security number, or full credit card info via an unsecured channel 4.
    • Beware of Phone Scams and Texts Too: Phishing isn’t only via email. Be cautious with unexpected phone calls claiming to be tech support or officials – if someone calls you out of the blue and asks to “fix” your computer or requests sensitive info, it’s likely a scam. The same goes for text messages ("SMiShing") that have strange links or ask you to urgently verify something. Treat those with the same skepticism as emails. If you get a text with a link, do not click it. If it claims to be your bank, open your banking app or site manually to check for alerts.
    • Use Spam Filters and Security Tools: Keep your email’s spam/junk filter on; it will catch many phishing emails. Still, spam filters aren’t perfect, so stay vigilant even with emails that land in your inbox. Some antivirus software and browser toolbars can warn about known phishing sites – pay attention if you see warnings (e.g., “This site is suspected of phishing, are you sure you want to continue?”).
    • “Think Before You Click”: Always take a moment and stop to think before clicking any link or attachment – this simple habit can save you from many scams 4. If something feels even slightly off or too good to be true, it probably is. Trust your instincts and verify authenticity through a separate channel. No legitimate tech support or official will ever contact you unsolicited and ask for your login credentials or remote access to your device 4 – if someone does, it’s a huge red flag.
    • What to Do If You Suspect a Phish: If you encounter a suspicious email, do not reply to it. You can report it (for example, forward phishing emails to or to your local cybersecurity center) and then delete it 8. If you did click a link or download a file and suspect it might be malicious, disconnect from the internet and run a full antivirus scan immediately. Then change any passwords that might have been compromised. If you gave out financial info, contact your bank. The faster you act, the better you can mitigate any damage.
(Checklist: Unsolicited message = suspicion ✓ No clicking unknown links/attachments ✓ Verify via known contacts ✓ Recognize scare tactics ✓ Never give out passwords or codes ✓) 1 47. Use Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)
Why: Multi-factor authentication adds an extra layer of security to your logins. Even if someone steals your password, they can’t get into your account without the second factor. It dramatically reduces the chance of account compromise.
    • Enable MFA on Important Accounts: For any account that offers it, especially your email, banking/financial accounts, social media, and other sensitive services, turn on multi-factor authentication (MFA) 4. MFA (also known as two-factor authentication or 2FA) means you need a second step to log in, such as a one-time code from your phone or a fingerprint, in addition to your password. Common forms of MFA include:
        ◦ Authenticator app codes (e.g., using an app like Google Authenticator or Authy to generate a 6-digit code).
        ◦ SMS text message codes (a code texted to your phone).
        ◦ Email codes or links sent to a secondary email (less secure than the above, but sometimes used).
        ◦ Biometrics (fingerprint or facial recognition).
        ◦ Physical security keys (a USB or NFC device like YubiKey – often for advanced users).
Enabling any form of MFA is far better than password alone. Many services have a security settings section where you can “turn on 2-Step Verification”; follow their instructions (usually involves scanning a QR code with an authenticator app or confirming your phone number)
11. Once enabled, you will typically enter a code from your phone each time you log in from a new device.
    • MFA Protects You If Passwords Are Stolen: With MFA in place, stealing your password isn’t enough for a hacker to access your account 11. For example, if your email password leaked in a breach, an attacker still couldn’t log in without that second factor (which only you possess). This stops the vast majority of automated account hacking attempts. According to cybersecurity agencies, using MFA can block over 99% of bulk phishing attacks because the attacker would need your physical device or biometric too.
    • Prioritize Email and Financial Accounts: Start with your primary email account – it’s often the gateway to reset other passwords. Make sure MFA is on for your email 4. Do the same for bank accounts, investment accounts, shopping sites (like Amazon, PayPal), and any account that holds personal or payment information. Social media accounts should have MFA as well, to prevent hijacking. Even many gaming services, cloud storage (Dropbox, Google Drive), and others offer MFA options in settings. It might take a few minutes to set each one up, but it dramatically improves security for each account.
    • Use an Authenticator App or Security Key When Possible: If given a choice, authenticator apps or physical keys are generally more secure than SMS texts (SMS can be vulnerable to SIM-swapping attacks). However, SMS codes are still far better than nothing, so use them if they’re the only option. Some services will even allow you to set up multiple methods (for example, SMS plus an authenticator app as backup). Follow the service’s guidance – for instance, the Australian Cyber Security Centre provides links on how to set up 2FA on popular services 9.
    • Protect Your Second Factor: When you enable MFA, most services will provide backup codes – save those in a safe place (write them down and lock away, or store in a password manager) in case you lose your device. Never share your authentication codes with anyone. Be wary of phishing messages that ask for your 2FA code; a legitimate company will never ask you to read a one-time code to them. That code is only for you to input into the service’s login page.
    • Make MFA a Habit: Whenever you sign up for a new service, check if it supports 2FA/MFA (look under security settings for “two-step verification”). It’s become quite common, and enabling it from the start is easier than doing it later. Many platforms even prompt you to enable it – take advantage of that. It might add a few seconds to your login process, but it greatly boosts your account security 11.
(Checklist: MFA enabled on email ✓ MFA on bank/financial accounts ✓ MFA on major social/commerce accounts ✓ Authenticator app configured ✓ Backup codes saved ✓) 11 48. Safe Browsing Habits
Why: Safe browsing is about using the internet carefully so that you avoid malware and scams. By being mindful of the sites you visit and how you interact with web content, you can steer clear of many threats.
    • Check for HTTPS (Secure Connections): Only enter personal information or login credentials on websites that have a secure connection. Look for the padlock icon and “https\://” in the address bar. The “s” means the connection is encrypted. For example, your bank’s website or any online checkout page should be HTTPS, which helps ensure you’re on the legitimate site and that your data is encrypted in transit. (But note: the padlock alone doesn’t guarantee a site is legitimate, but no padlock is a definite red flag on any site asking for passwords or payments.)
    • Avoid Shady Websites and Downloads: Be cautious when visiting websites you’re not familiar with, especially if they are offering premium content (like movies, software, or music) for “free” – these could be malware traps. Sites that are filled with pop-up ads or ask you to download a strange file to view content should be avoided. Do not download software from unofficial sources or peer-to-peer networks, as those files often hide malware. If you need a particular software or file, get it from the official website or a well-known app store. Pirated software or cracks almost always come bundled with malicious programs.
    • Pay Attention to Browser Warnings: Modern web browsers will warn you if you’re about to visit a known malicious site (for example, you might see a big red warning page saying the site is deceptive or poses a security risk). Heed those warnings and do not proceed 4. Similarly, if you try to download a file and your browser or antivirus says the file may be harmful, think twice before overriding. Those warnings exist because other users or scanning engines have identified problems.
    • Use Bookmarks and Trusted URLs: For sites you use frequently (like online banking, email, shopping), it’s safer to navigate to them directly rather than via email links or search engine ads. Bookmark the correct URL and use that bookmark every time 1. This helps you avoid typo-squatting sites (fake sites with URLs similar to the real one) or phishing links. If you must search for a site, be sure the result is the official site (watch out for “Ad” results or slight misspellings). Creating bookmarks for important sites ensures you always land in the right place 1.
    • Log Out and Secure Your Sessions: When using a public or shared computer (like in a library or friend’s house), always log out of websites when you’re done, especially email or social media. Also close the browser window. This prevents the next user from accessing your accounts. Even on your own device, it’s not a bad practice to log out of sensitive sites when finished, in case someone else gains access to your device.
    • Avoid Using Admin Account for Daily Use: If possible, use a standard user account on your computer for everyday activities and have a separate admin account for installing software. This way, even if you accidentally click something malicious while browsing, it’s harder for it to make system-level changes without your admin password 7. On Windows, you can use a standard user and Windows will prompt for the admin password when needed. On a Mac, your daily account can be standard as well. This limits the damage malware can do.
    • Be Careful with Browser Extensions and Plugins: Only install browser add-ons from sources you trust, and keep them updated. Some malicious extensions can hijack your browser or insert ads/spyware. Do a periodic audit of your browser extensions and remove any you don’t absolutely need or those you don’t recognize. The same goes for plugins like Flash or Java (which are largely obsolete now) – if you don’t need them, uninstall them, as they have historically been targets for attacks.
    • Use Security Features in Your Browser: Enable features like “Safe Browsing” (in Chrome) or similar security filters which can block known bad websites. Most browsers also have a built-in password manager and “breach alerts” – for instance, they can warn you if a password you saved was found in a data breach. Consider using these features. Additionally, consider using content blockers or privacy extensions to reduce ads and scripts that could be malicious (as discussed in the privacy section, choose reputable ones).
    • Mind Your Downloads: When downloading files, stick to known websites. If you need freeware or shareware, use well-known repositories (like official app stores or sites like Ninite or Softpedia that check for malware). After downloading, if your system has an option to scan the file for viruses, do so. On Windows, right-click and “Scan with Windows Defender” for example.
    • Secure Browsing on Public Computers: If you ever use a public computer (say at an internet cafe or hotel business center), assume that system could be compromised. Avoid accessing sensitive accounts on such machines. But if you must, use the browser’s incognito/private mode and never save passwords when prompted. And as mentioned, log out and close the browser when done. It’s also wise to change those passwords later, just in case the public machine was logging keystrokes.
(Checklist: Prefer HTTPS sites ✓ Avoid untrusted downloads ✓ Follow browser safety warnings ✓ Use bookmarks for bank/login sites ✓ Log out on shared devices ✓ Minimal browser extensions ✓) 19. Social Media Safety
Why: Social media is a public-facing window into your life. If not carefully managed, it can expose a lot of personal information to strangers or malicious actors. By using privacy settings and being mindful of what you share, you can enjoy social media more safely.
    • Lock Down Privacy Settings: Use the privacy settings on each social platform to control who can see your posts, photos, and personal info 9. For example, set your Facebook profile to private so only friends can see your full details, or make your Instagram account private if you wish to approve followers. On platforms like Twitter (X), consider whether you want your tweets protected (approved followers only). Review the settings for things like who can look you up by email/phone number, and opt out if you don’t want to be easily found. The NCSC recommends using privacy settings across all platforms to “manage your digital footprint” 9, ensuring only intended people see your content.
    • Think Before You Post: Assume that anything you post publicly could be permanent 4. Even if you delete a post, someone might have saved it or it may be archived. So before sharing, ask yourself: Would I be okay if this information was seen by a stranger (or broadcast to the world)? 4. If not, refrain or adjust your audience settings. Also think how a bad actor could use something you share. Posting details like your mother’s maiden name, your pet’s name, your birthday or birthplace might seem harmless, but those are often answers to common security questions 4! A criminal gathering pieces of your personal puzzle from social media could potentially impersonate you or guess passwords. Avoid oversharing personal details such as your home address, phone number, full birth date, vacation plans, or other identifying info in public posts 4. For example, you might share your birthday with month and day but leave out the year (to reduce identity theft risk), or share vacation photos after you return rather than broadcasting that your house is empty for two weeks.
    • Limit Who Can Contact You: Adjust settings to limit friend or follow requests to people who have some connection (like “friends of friends” on Facebook). Be wary of accepting friend requests or DMs from people you don’t know in real life. Scammers often create fake profiles to befriend people and gain their trust or information 9. If you get a request from someone you know but you’re already connected, it could be an imposter account – double check with your friend via another medium.
    • Beware of Social Engineering and Scams on Social: Not all threats come via email – scammers also operate on social media. Be cautious of random messages, even if they appear to come from a friend. If a friend’s account sends you a strange link or asks for money, verify through another channel (call or text them) because their account might be hacked 9. Don’t fall for messages from strangers claiming “I found this video of you, check it out” – that’s a common trick to get you to click a malicious link. And never agree to send money or personal info to someone you met online without absolute verification; romance and friendship scams are prevalent on social platforms.
    • Avoid Quizzes and Games That Ask for Too Much Info: Those fun quizzes like “What kind of animal are you?” that ask you to grant access to your profile or that require lots of personal answers could be mining data. Likewise, be careful with third-party apps that connect to your social media account; only authorize reputable ones. Periodically review and revoke access for apps you no longer use or that you don’t recognize. Facebook and others have a settings page for apps connected to your account – prune it regularly.
    • Use Two-Factor Authentication on Social Accounts: As mentioned earlier, enable MFA on your social media logins too. This prevents someone who guesses or steals your password from hijacking your account and, say, DM'ing your friends scams or posting as you. It’s especially important for accounts with many contacts or that you use to sign into other services.
    • Keep Personal and Professional Separate: If possible, don’t share work-related sensitive info on your personal social media. And vice versa, be mindful of what personal info you share on professional networks like LinkedIn. Scammers can use personal details to answer account recovery questions or craft convincing phishing emails. Treat your online persona as part of your security surface – manage it actively.
    • Children’s Privacy: If you have kids or younger family members online, educate them about privacy and consider stricter controls on their accounts. Many social platforms have minimum age requirements and parental controls. Ensure they know not to share things like their school, daily routine, or photos with identifiable details publicly.
(Checklist: Profile info private to friends ✓ Don’t post sensitive personal data ✓ Verify strange messages even from friends ✓ Be picky with friend requests ✓ Audit third-party app access ✓) 4 910. Mobile Device Security
Why: Our smartphones and tablets contain a wealth of personal data (messages, photos, banking apps, emails) and often stay logged into accounts. They’re also portable and easy to lose. Securing your mobile devices is just as important as your computers.
    • Use a Strong Lock Screen: Lock your phone or tablet with a PIN, password, or biometric lock so that if it’s lost or stolen, a stranger can’t access your data 4. Avoid easy PINs like “1234” or patterns that are simple (like a straight line). A 6-digit PIN or an alphanumeric passcode is best. Many devices allow a fingerprint or face unlock – these are convenient and generally secure (though still have a PIN/password as backup). Ensure your device is set to auto-lock quickly (e.g., after 1 or 2 minutes of inactivity) so it isn’t sitting unlocked if you forget to turn off the screen 4.
    • Keep Your Mobile OS and Apps Updated: Just like computers, phones need regular updates to fix security issues 4. Enable automatic updates for your phone’s operating system and for apps. iOS and Android frequently release patches – install them promptly. App stores will update your apps; set them to auto-update over Wi-Fi. Cybercriminals can exploit vulnerabilities in old versions of mobile OS or apps just as on PCs.
    • Only Install Apps from Trusted Sources: Stick to the official app stores (Apple App Store for iPhone/iPad, Google Play Store for Android) when downloading apps 1. Do not jailbreak or “sideload” apps from unofficial sources, as those are not vetted and could contain malware. Both Apple and Google review apps in their stores to filter out malicious ones (though not perfect, it’s much safer than random APK files from the web) 1. On Android, if you ever enable the setting to install from unknown sources, turn it back off immediately after installing the specific app you needed (and be absolutely sure that app is safe).
    • Review App Permissions and Privacy: As mentioned in the privacy section, check what data your mobile apps can access 4. For example, some apps might unnecessarily request your location or contacts. On both Android and iOS, you can view permissions by app and disable those that seem excessive (for instance, you can prevent a game from accessing your GPS or a photo-editing app from reading your contacts). Grant permissions on a need-to-use basis; you can allow GPS access “Only while using the app” instead of “Always,” for instance 4. Also, be mindful when an app asks for new permissions after an update – if a simple app suddenly wants access to something new without a good reason, reconsider keeping that app.
    • Enable Remote Find and Wipe: Make sure you have features like “Find My iPhone” (Apple) or “Find My Device” (Android) enabled 4. These services allow you to locate your device on a map if it’s lost, remotely make it ring, or even wipe the device’s data if you suspect it’s stolen and unlikely to be recovered. Having this set up beforehand is a lifesaver in case of loss. Practice using it (you can go to the web interface to see how it works) so you’re familiar with the process.
    • Avoid Public Charging Ports (“Juice Jacking”): Be careful when charging your phone in public places like airports or malls. Public USB charging stations could be hacked to inject malware or steal data from devices (this is rare, but possible) 4. It’s safer to use your own charger plugged into an electrical outlet, or use a portable battery pack. If you must use a public USB port, consider a USB data blocker (a little adapter that allows only power, not data transfer). Likewise, don’t plug in random USB drives or cables into your phone. Use your own charging cable – there have been cases of modified cables that can compromise devices.
    • Turn Off Unused Wireless Connections: Keep your phone’s Bluetooth and Wi-Fi turned off when you’re not actively using them 4. This reduces the risk of attackers exploiting those interfaces. Also disable any setting that automatically connects to open Wi-Fi networks – you don’t want your phone randomly joining any network in range. It’s better to manually choose networks. When in public, leaving Bluetooth on visible mode could allow unknown devices to send requests to your phone.
    • Treat Your Phone Like Your Wallet: Because in a sense, it is. Never leave your mobile device unattended in public. Keep it with you or locked away. Physical theft is a common threat. If you store sensitive information (like passwords, 2FA codes, or banking apps) on the device, someone who gets physical access could attempt to break in. Always assume a lost phone in unlocked state is a worst-case scenario – thus the emphasis on having a strong lock and remote wipe.
    • Encrypt Your Device Storage: Most modern phones encrypt their storage by default (iOS devices and newer Androids do this). If you have an older Android phone, ensure encryption is turned on in settings. This way, if someone tries to extract data from the phone’s memory directly, it will be unreadable without your passcode.
    • Backup Your Mobile Data: As part of device security, keep your phone data backed up (either to the cloud or to a computer). That way if you do have to remote-wipe it or it gets irrecoverably lost, you can restore your contacts, photos, and apps to a new device with minimal loss.

Conclusion & Next Steps: By following this comprehensive checklist – from strong passwords and MFA, to regular updates, backups, and practicing good judgment online – you are performing your own personal cybersecurity health check. These steps, drawn from expert recommendations, dramatically lower your risk of cyber incidents like account hacking, malware infections, or identity theft 1. Cybersecurity is an ongoing process, so it’s wise to stay informed about new threats and periodically re-check your settings and practices. Many government cyber agencies offer free alert services to notify you of emerging scams or vulnerabilities 4; consider signing up for a reputable one in your country to stay in the loop.
Remember, you don’t have to be an IT expert to be safe online – basic cyber hygiene and cautious habits go a long way 4. Keep this checklist handy and review it occasionally. Encourage your family and friends to do these steps as well, since security is stronger when everyone is savvy. By taking these proactive measures, you’re protecting your personal information, your finances, and your peace of mind. Stay safe out there! 4
