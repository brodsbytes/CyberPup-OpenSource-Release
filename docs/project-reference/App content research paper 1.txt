CyberPup Cybersecurity Education Plan
Empowering Everyday People to Stay Safe Online

Introduction
Everyday people are increasingly reliant on smartphones, computers, and the internet for daily tasks – from banking and shopping to communicating with loved ones. Unfortunately, cybercriminals know this and actively target individuals who have little technical know-how. Nearly one-third of Americans experienced some form of online financial fraud in 20231, and global cybercrime losses are soaring into the tens of billions of dollars annually2. Yet many users mistakenly believe they’re “not worthwhile targets” for hackers3. This false sense of security, coupled with low awareness, leaves regular people vulnerable.
CyberPup is envisioned as a friendly “cyber health check” app that guides non-technical users through securing their digital lives. The goal is to cover all facets of cyber threats an everyday person might face – from email hacks to bank account fraud – in a structured, beginner-friendly way. Each topic will be broken down into bite-sized lessons with simple language, relatable examples, and frequent positive feedback (much like Khan Academy’s step-by-step learning approach). The app will be product-agnostic, meaning it educates users on best practices without pushing specific brands – instead, it will suggest generic solutions (e.g. using any reputable password manager that fits the user’s device).
By completing CyberPup’s guided courses, even a tech novice should feel confident and empowered knowing they’ve significantly improved their cybersecurity posture. The sections below outline: (1) the biggest cybersecurity risks to everyday users (based on reputable statistics), (2) recommended course categories to address those risks, and (3) design principles for delivering the content in an engaging, beginner-friendly manner (positive reinforcement, small goals, etc.).

Biggest Cybersecurity Risks for Everyday People
Everyday users face a wide range of cyber threats and bad digital habits. Understanding these common risks is crucial, since our course content will be structured around mitigating each one. According to FBI reports and industry studies, the most prevalent dangers for individuals include phishing scams, weak passwords, malware attacks, identity theft, and privacy breaches. Below are the key threat categories affecting regular people, with data on their impact:
    • Phishing and Email Scams: Phishing — fraudulent messages that trick you into revealing logins or personal info — is the single most reported cybercrime facing the general public1. In fact, phishing was the #1 incident type reported in 2022, more common than any other complaint1. Malicious emails are ubiquitous: an estimated 1.2% of all emails sent are malicious phishing emails, which works out to about 3.4 billion phishing emails every day44. These scams often masquerade as trusted institutions (banks, popular brands, even coworkers) to steal passwords, credit card numbers, or money. Falling for a phishing email can lead to an email account takeover (allowing hackers to reset your other account passwords via your inbox) or direct financial loss. Email remains the top delivery method for malware as well – roughly 94% of malware arrives via email attachments or links5. This means an everyday user’s inbox is arguably the biggest digital danger zone. Besides email, phishing also comes via text messages (“smishing”) and phone calls (“vishing”), such as tech support scams where fraudsters call pretending to be from Microsoft or Apple. All told, phishing and related social engineering scams are the first thing our users need to recognize and guard against.
    • Weak or Reused Passwords: Poor password habits are another huge risk area for non-tech-savvy people. A staggering 81% of data breaches are caused by weak or reused passwords according to Verizon’s security research6. Most people reuse passwords across many sites – one global survey found 85% of respondents admitted to reusing passwords on multiple accounts7. This means if one account gets hacked (say, via a data breach or phishing), attackers can try the same password elsewhere and break into other accounts – a domino effect called “credential stuffing.” Unfortunately, many users still choose easily guessable passwords or variants of “password” and their names; 52% include personal names or common words in their passwords7. It’s no surprise then that nearly half of Americans have had at least one password compromised in the past year. Weak passwords are basically an open door for hackers – like using the same key for every lock. Because stolen passwords are behind so many account hacks, improving password security (using strong, unique passwords and two-factor authentication) will be a cornerstone of our courses.
    • Malware and Unsecured Devices: Malware refers to malicious software (viruses, trojans, ransomware, spyware, etc.) that can infect your device. Everyday users often get malware by downloading unknown email attachments or software or by visiting compromised websites. Without proper safeguards, a person might unknowingly install a trojan app that steals their data or a ransomware virus that locks their files for ransom. Mobile devices are increasingly targeted: cybersecurity researchers have noted a rise in malware disguised as legitimate apps on official app stores, aimed at infecting smartphones3. Additionally, running outdated software (missing critical security updates) is a common vulnerability that attackers exploit3. For example, if you never update your computer or phone, known security holes remain open. Many individuals also lack antivirus/anti-malware protection or don’t use the firewall on their home network, making malware infections more likely. The impact of malware on an individual can be devastating – a ransomware attack on a personal computer might demand hundreds of dollars to get your files back3, and a spyware infection could silently steal your banking logins. This risk category highlights the need for device security practices like software updates, antivirus use, and careful app installs.
    • Financial Fraud & Identity Theft: Financial cybercrime against individuals is rampant. Attackers seek to hijack bank accounts, steal credit card numbers, or commit identity theft (using someone’s personal information to open new accounts or loans). Almost one in three Americans was affected by some form of online financial fraud in 20231 – including credit/debit card fraud, fraudulent money transfers, etc. The Federal Trade Commission (FTC) reports that among identity theft cases in 2024, credit card fraud (opening new credit accounts in someone’s name) was the most common form – accounting for 44% of identity theft reports2. Another 32% were “miscellaneous” identity theft involving things like online shopping fraud and email or social media account fraud2. In practice, this means millions of people each year have their personal and financial information misused. A “bank account hack” could result from an attacker obtaining your online banking password (perhaps via phishing or a data breach) or installing malware that logs your keystrokes. The consequences are immediate and serious: thieves may drain your bank balance or rack up charges on your credit cards. Beyond direct monetary loss, identity theft can damage your credit and take months to straighten out. We see enormous monetary impacts – for example, Americans reported $12.5 billion lost to cybercrime in 2023 (much of that from fraudulent financial schemes)8. Everyday people need to learn how to spot financial scams, secure their banking access, and respond quickly if compromise is suspected.
    • Social Media and Privacy Risks: Many users (including those “with very little understanding of technology”) are active on social media or store personal data online, often unaware of the privacy and security implications. Oversharing personal details on Facebook or Instagram can expose someone to identity theft or targeted scams – e.g. a hacker gleaning your mother’s maiden name, pet’s name, or birthdate (common security question answers) from social profiles. There’s also the risk of social media account takeovers if those accounts have weak passwords or no two-factor protection – a hacker could hijack your Facebook and then scam your friends with it. Social media is a common vector for cyberattacks, identity theft, and even reputational damage9. For instance, phishing isn’t limited to email; attackers often send Facebook messages or Twitter DMs with malicious links. Moreover, the interactive nature of social networks can spread malware (e.g. a malicious link that, when clicked, tries to infect your device and then automatically messages all your contacts). Privacy settings on these platforms are frequently overlooked by casual users, which can leave posts or personal info visible to “friends of friends” or the public. Another aspect is phone-based scams and impostor calls (“Hi, I’m calling from your bank/Internet provider...”) which rely on personal info often found online to sound convincing – these fall under social engineering too. In short, without guidance, everyday users may inadvertently leak sensitive information or fall prey to scams via the social platforms and communication channels they use daily.
Why These Risks Matter: Unlike large companies, individuals don’t have IT departments to back them up – one mistake like clicking a bad link or using a weak password can have direct, painful consequences. Personal victims of cybercrime often suffer financial loss, stress, and major inconvenience. For example, if a hacker gets into your primary email, they can reset passwords to all your other accounts in a domino effect; if ransomware locks your family photos, you might lose them forever without backups; if a fraudster empties your bank account or runs up your credit card, you’re left scrambling to recover funds. And while a corporation might weather a cyberattack, an individual might be hit much harder – studies have shown many people cannot easily recover from sudden losses (the average ransom demand of about $1,000 could mean choosing between paying the hacker or paying your rent)3. Recognizing these everyday threats is the first step. CyberPup’s content will directly address each of these risk areas, teaching users how to defend themselves against phishing, use strong passwords, secure their devices, protect their finances, and guard their privacy. The next section outlines how we will structure the app’s courses around these themes.

Course Categories and Content for CyberPup
To effectively cover all facets of cyber threats for everyday people, we propose organizing CyberPup’s curriculum into a set of clear, beginner-friendly categories. Each category corresponds to a major risk area identified above and will contain one or more courses or modules that walk the user through improving that aspect of their cybersecurity. The focus throughout is on practical steps (the “health check” actions users can take) delivered in plain language. We also ensure product-agnostic advice – recommending types of solutions rather than specific brands, with platform-specific tips only where appropriate (for instance, guiding iPhone users toward built-in iOS features and Android users toward equivalent solutions available to them).
Below is an overview of the key course categories we recommend for CyberPup, along with the core topics each will cover:


Each of these five categories will contain a structured mini-course (or multiple submodules) that guides the user through the topic. Here’s a closer look at what each course entails:
1. Password Security & Authentication
Why it’s important: Weak or reused passwords are a leading cause of account hacks for individuals. As noted, an overwhelming 81% of hacking-related breaches involve stolen or poor passwords6. Teaching users how to improve their passwords provides huge security gains with relatively little effort or technical complexity. This course will likely be the starting point in the app, since securing passwords creates a foundation for all other protections.
Course content: This module will cover:
    • Creating Strong Passwords: Educate users on what makes a password strong (length, mix of characters, avoiding dictionary words/personal info). We’ll illustrate how a weak password like “abcd1234” can be cracked in seconds, whereas a 12-character passphrase is far safer. If available, we might show a statistic or simple graphic about password strength (for example, that “123456” has been the most common leaked password in data breaches). We’ll emphasize unique passwords for each account – explaining with an analogy (“using the same password everywhere is like using one key for your house, car, and office – if it’s lost, everything is compromised”). The shocking fact that 85% of people reuse passwords7 will be used to motivate change.
    • Using a Password Manager: For many beginners, the idea of remembering dozens of complex passwords seems impossible. We introduce password managers as the solution: a secure app or built-in phone feature that remembers and even generates strong passwords for you. It will be stressed that users don’t have to buy anything – there are free, trusted options and even built-in managers. For instance, we’ll explain that iOS devices have iCloud Keychain (Apple’s built-in password management) which can suggest strong passwords and autofill them, and that Android/Chrome users have Google’s Password Manager, or they can use reputable free apps like Bitwarden (open-source) or paid ones like 1Password/LastPass, etc. The key is we remain agnostic: we give a couple of safe choices per platform and let the user pick what they’re comfortable with. The course might include a quick demo (screenshots or a short video snippet) of how to use a password manager on a phone – e.g., how to store a password and retrieve it – to prove it’s not too hard. We will reassure users about security of these tools, and also mention not writing passwords on sticky notes or saving them in plain Notepad files, which some might be doing.
    • Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA): We’ll introduce MFA (also called 2FA – two-factor authentication) as an extra layer beyond just the password. This is critical because even a strong password could be stolen in a data breach without the user doing anything wrong. MFA means you need a second step (a one-time code on your phone, or an approval prompt) to log in, so a hacker with just your password still can’t get in. The course will highlight that enabling 2FA on important accounts (email, bank, social media) dramatically reduces the chance of takeover. We can cite that even though it’s an extra step, 2FA is increasingly common: 66% of people now use two-factor auth on personal accounts7. We’ll keep instructions very simple: e.g., “We’ll show you how to turn on 2FA on a sample account” (maybe a generic example or linking out to popular sites’ instructions in a resource section). The app can’t directly configure their 2FA, but it can provide step-by-step guidance (with screenshots) for, say, Google/Gmail’s 2FA setup as a real-world example, encouraging them to do similar for their own accounts. This fosters a sense of achievement – it might even be the first “task” the user accomplishes: enabling 2FA somewhere and getting congratulated by the app.
    • Avoiding Common Pitfalls: We’ll wrap up the password module with a few DON’Ts in beginner terms: don’t reuse passwords (we can show them how one leak can cascade, referencing real incidents briefly), don’t share your password with others (except in a very secure way if absolutely needed – though for personal accounts, sharing should be avoided entirely). And importantly, never give your password or verification codes to anyone who asks via email or phone – legitimate companies will not ask for those; if someone is asking, it’s a scam. This ties into phishing awareness but is worth reiterating here.
Outcome: After completing this course, users will understand how to change weak passwords to strong ones, will ideally start using a password manager (or at least Apple’s/Google’s built-in features), and will enable 2FA on key accounts. The app can have a checklist like: “Did you secure your email password and turn on 2FA? ✅ Great job!” to reinforce progress. Since password security underpins all other aspects, we expect this segment to provide an immediate boost to their overall “cyber health score.” In fact, by doing these steps, an individual likely eliminates the biggest risk of easy account hacking in their life: remember, most breaches won’t happen if attackers can’t crack or reuse your passwords6.
2. Phishing & Scam Awareness
Why it’s important: Phishing is the front door of most cyber-attacks against everyday people. It doesn’t matter how strong your password is if you accidentally give it away to a fake website. As noted, phishing is the most commonly reported internet crime and accounts for a large share of incidents1. By training users to spot scams, we empower them to avoid the majority of traps set by cybercriminals. This course will be highly interactive, as recognizing phishing often involves looking at examples.
Course content: Key topics in this module:
    • Recognizing Phishing Emails: We will show users examples of what phishing looks like. For instance, an email claiming “Your bank account is locked, click here to verify” or “Package delivery issue, open attachment for invoice”. We’ll point out common red flags: poor grammar, unknown sender addresses, urgent or threatening language (“act now or your account will be closed”), and mismatched links (hovering over a link shows a different URL). We’ll also demonstrate how phishing emails often spoof familiar brands. According to research, phishing sites often use well-known names like Amazon, Google, or Netflix to appear legitimate4 – we can mention that if an email uses those names and asks for sensitive info, you should be suspicious. The course might include a mini-quiz: show two email screenshots and ask the user to pick which one is the phish. Immediate feedback will explain the clues. This kind of interactive learning is beginner-friendly and confidence-building (“Ah, I can spot these!”). We should incorporate the statistic that phishing accounts for about 20–36% of data breaches (various reports)4 to drive home its prevalence. Also, advice like: never click links in unsolicited emails – instead, navigate to the official website yourself or call the company if in doubt.
    • Smishing and Vishing: We won’t limit to email – the course also covers scam text messages and phone calls, since many everyday users encounter those. We’ll described smishing (SMS/text phishing) – e.g. fake package delivery texts with malicious links, or “Your bank detected unusual login, click here \[phishy link]”. The same skepticism should apply: if you get an unexpected alarming text, don’t click the link; instead log in to your account via your app or site that you trust. For vishing (voice phishing), the classic example is the tech support scam call. We will role-play or describe a scenario: “An unknown caller claims to be from Microsoft and says you have a virus, then tries to convince you to install an app or give them remote access to ‘fix’ it.” Users will learn that legitimate tech companies do not call you out of the blue to fix issues; those calls are always scams. Another common vishing scenario is someone pretending to be from your bank’s fraud department asking for verification codes – again, banks say they never ask for those codes over the phone. We can share that tech support scams were the #1 fraud reported by older adults in recent FBI data8 (to emphasize how widespread it is), but also clarify anyone can be targeted by such calls.
    • Safe Browsing & Avoiding Deception: Phishing often directs users to fake websites that look real. We teach a few basics of safe web browsing to avoid falling for this: e.g., always check the URL of a site before entering credentials or personal info. Does it match the official site (correct spelling, proper domain)? Modern browsers also highlight if the connection is secure (HTTPS padlock) – while a padlock alone doesn’t guarantee legitimacy, absence of it is a red flag if you’re about to log in. We might show a side-by-side of a real vs fake login page for, say, Gmail, pointing out differences in URL or design. Another tip: if you receive an email with a link, one strategy is to not use that link at all, but independently go to the website (e.g., type your bank’s address yourself) to verify if there’s actually an issue. Essentially, “stop and think” before clicking. We’ll incorporate positive reinforcement for skeptical behavior – e.g. praising the user for choosing to verify independently rather than trusting a random message.
    • Common Consumer Scams: Beyond online messages, we’ll list other top scams targeting everyday folks: examples include online shopping scams (fake e-commerce sites or sellers that never deliver product – which correspond to that “non-payment/non-delivery” category, the #3 most reported issue1), lottery or prize scams (“You won a prize, just pay this small fee”), and impersonation scams (someone pretending to be a friend or relative in need of money, which often happens via email or social media account takeovers). We don’t want to scare users with every fraud under the sun, but a general awareness that “if it sounds too good to be true, it probably is” and “verify identities of people who ask you for money or info” is valuable. We’ll instruct never to send sensitive info like passwords, OTP codes, or bank details over email or text – legitimate services won’t ask for those via those channels.
    • What to Do if You Suspect a Scam: The course will also empower users with steps to take if they think they’ve encountered phishing. For example: if you receive a suspicious email, you can report it (many email providers allow reporting spam/phish which helps the community). If you clicked a bad link or entered your password on a fake site, don’t panic – immediately change that password and any other account that used the same password (though hopefully by now they won’t be reusing passwords due to Course #1!). If you were tricked into giving financial info, contact your bank ASAP. These “what now?” steps will be provided in a calm, reassuring tone – mistakes can happen, but quick action can limit damage.
Outcome: After this module, users should be significantly better at spotting and avoiding scams. We will likely include a final interactive quiz or “phishing simulation” in-app to let them test their skills in a safe environment. Many organizations use phishing simulations to train employees; for our everyday users, a simplified version could be both fun and educational. By completing this, users earn a badge like “Phishing Detective” or a pop-up that says “Excellent – you’re now savvy about scams!” which adds to their confidence. This training is critical because, as one stat puts it, phishing scams are responsible for roughly 1 in 5 data breaches4 – if they can avoid phishing, they dodge a huge portion of threats. The app’s tone here will be positive and empowering: instead of making users afraid of every email, we make them feel capable of sniffing out bad ones. This directly reduces their chance of falling victim to email hacks and related cybercrimes.
3. Device & Network Security
Why it’s important: Even if you have great passwords and never fall for scams, an insecure device can still get compromised. This category covers the “health” of the user’s devices (their smartphone, laptop, tablet, home network) – analogous to locking your doors and keeping your car well-maintained. Everyday people often overlook basic device hygiene: they might ignore software updates, have no antivirus, use default settings, or connect to any Wi-Fi they find. Hackers, however, exploit unpatched software and unsecured networks to deliver malware or steal data. Given that unpatched systems and misconfigured settings are common causes of security incidents5, we need to guide users through securing their hardware and connections.
Course content: Focus areas include:
    • Software Updates (Patching): We will strongly encourage enabling automatic updates on all devices. Many novices click “remind me later” endlessly when update prompts appear, not realizing those updates often fix critical security holes. The course will explain in simple terms: Software companies frequently find and fix security vulnerabilities – but if you don’t update, attackers can abuse those known weaknesses. For instance, we might say “Using an old version of your phone’s operating system is like leaving a known hole in your fence that burglars are aware of.” We’ll provide how-tos: e.g., show how to turn on auto-updates on Windows, on macOS, on Android (for system and apps via Play Store), and that iPhones by default update automatically when plugged in overnight (if not, how to check). The concept of “patch Tuesday” or similar isn’t necessary to mention, just “updates = good and usually seamless”. We could cite a stat like “Outdated applications cause X% of breaches” if available; if not, we have Kaspersky’s note that mobile attacks are rising via trojanized apps on app stores3 which underscores using updated, official apps.
    • Antivirus/Anti-Malware Protection: Explain the role of antivirus software in guarding against viruses, spyware, ransomware, etc. Many everyday users either don’t have any antivirus (especially on older PCs or Android devices) or rely on whatever came with the device. We will clarify that nowadays, most devices have some built-in protection – for example, Windows has Microsoft Defender which is pretty good when kept updated; macOS has some malware safeguards; Android has Google Play Protect. The course will suggest that at minimum they ensure those built-ins are active and updated. We’ll also discuss optional third-party antivirus for those who want extra peace of mind (but again, not pushing a product – perhaps advising to research well-reviewed solutions if they choose to install one). The user interface can have a small checklist like “Do you have antivirus enabled? Yes/No/Not sure – here’s how to check.” If they say no, we could have a tip screen: “On Windows, you can turn on Microsoft Defender in Security Settings. On Mac, ensure Gatekeeper is on. On Android, stick to Google Play Store for apps, as it reduces risk.” We can mention that malware attacks on individuals are common (for instance, “every day, over 300,000 new malicious programs are detected” – a stat often cited by security companies), but don’t overwhelm with big numbers. The main message: having an active anti-malware shield and keeping it updated greatly reduces the chance that a virus will successfully run on your device.
    • Firewall and Network Security: For home users, their Wi-Fi router and network settings are often weak links. We’ll advise a few simple steps: ensure your home Wi-Fi is password-protected (WPA2/WPA3 encryption) – most are, but some might still use default or no passwords. We might say “Check that you did change the default Wi-Fi password that came with your router; if not or unsure, here’s how to do it.” Not too deep into IT admin, just the basics anyone can follow from their router’s quick guide. Also, explain what a firewall is in non-technical terms: “a digital gatekeeper for your internet connection”. The course will encourage keeping the firewall on (Windows and macOS have one built-in; many routers also have firewall features by default). We might quote something like “a firewall is your first line of defense against unauthorized access”10 to give it legitimacy. For beginners, just knowing not to disable these things (many don’t even know what a firewall is, so we assure them it works quietly in the background).
    • Safe Use of Public Wi-Fi: This is a scenario-based subtopic. Many people connect to free Wi-Fi at cafés, airports, etc., which can be risky if the network is unsecured or potentially spoofed. We’ll teach users not to do sensitive transactions (like banking) on public Wi-Fi unless they use extra protection like a VPN. We explain that criminals can potentially “eavesdrop” on public Wi-Fi traffic to steal information (the classic coffee shop hacker scenario). The course will give practical tips: if you must use public Wi-Fi, avoid logging into important accounts, and consider using a reputable VPN app to encrypt your connection10. If the user doesn’t know what a VPN is, we define it simply (a tool to create an encrypted tunnel for your data – our Norton reference had a definition10). We will remain product-agnostic here too: mention that some browsers (like Opera) have free VPN, and both iOS and Android support various free or paid VPN apps – but also caution that not all VPNs are equally trustworthy10. The bottom line: home or cellular networks are safer; public Wi-Fi is for casual browsing only, or use a VPN if available.
    • Downloading Apps & Files Safely: Emphasize only downloading apps from official app stores (Google Play, Apple App Store) to greatly reduce risk of malware. Sideloading apps or downloading software from random websites is dangerous for novices. On computers, only download software from trusted publishers. We might recount how malware often masquerades as free games, music, or even fake antivirus – so stick to known legitimate sources. Also, advise caution when opening email attachments, even from people you know, especially if you weren’t expecting anything – their account might have been compromised and sending malware. This ties back to phishing but also is a device hygiene practice.
    • Data Backup: A final piece in device security is ensuring that even if all else fails (say you do get ransomware or your device crashes), you don’t lose everything. We’ll encourage regular backups of important data. For beginners, using cloud backup services or an external hard drive can be introduced. For example, suggest they turn on cloud backup for photos (Google Photos or iCloud Photos) and important documents (OneDrive/Google Drive). Or simply periodically copy files to a USB drive (and keep it somewhere safe). This isn’t a “security” measure per se, but it’s a safety net that turns a potentially catastrophic hack into a recoverable inconvenience. We can remind them of the earlier ransomware example: if you have your files backed up, you won't be tempted to pay a ransom to retrieve them3; you can just reset your device and restore your files.
Outcome: Upon completing this course, users will have their devices much more fortified. The app might walk them through a “device security checklist” interactively: e.g., “Check for system updates now” (with a button or instructions for each OS), “Is your antivirus on and updated?” (maybe even detect if the app can prompt an answer, or just guide the user to check), “Enable your firewall” (with how-to, if it was off), “Review your Wi-Fi router password,” etc. Each time they confirm a step, they get a little congratulatory note. This turns a potentially dry topic into a series of small wins. We will highlight that these behind-the-scenes defenses are crucial: for example, without updates and antivirus, the user is much more likely to get infected by malware (given billions of threats out there4). By doing things like updating and using antivirus, they cut that risk dramatically. We can cite that antivirus software can catch the vast majority of common malware before it does harm. And turning on auto-updates means they’re protected against known threats without even thinking about it. In essence, this course helps users develop good “digital hygiene” habits, analogous to locking doors and washing hands in the physical world.
4. Online Privacy & Social Media
Why it’s important: Many everyday users share a lot of personal information online without realizing how it might be used against them. This course helps users take control of their privacy settings and understand boundaries of what’s safe to share. By doing so, we lower the risk of social engineering attacks (which often rely on personal info) and protect against identity theft or harassment. Also, being privacy-conscious can prevent unintended exposure of one’s data (for example, a public social media post revealing your vacation dates could invite a physical burglary – an angle people might not consider).
Course content: Topics include:
    • Privacy Settings on Social Media: We’ll guide users through checking their privacy on platforms like Facebook, Instagram, and others. For example, on Facebook: ensure your profile isn’t fully public – maybe set posts to “Friends only”, review what information (phone number, email, birthday) is visible to whom, and enable features like login alerts for unrecognized logins. On Instagram, maybe have a private account if appropriate or at least understand that anyone can see a public post. Given the wide range of social platforms, we’ll focus on principles and perhaps use one platform as a tutorial example (Facebook being a common one). The Hut Six training insight notes that social media safety includes setting strong privacy settings and being cautious about sharing9. We will mirror that, telling users: “Take a moment to visit your profile settings and limit what strangers can see.” The app could have a mini-checklist or external links “How to change your privacy settings on popular sites” if providing that detail inline is too much.
    • Think Before You Share: This part is about encouraging a habit of assessing information sensitivity. We explain that seemingly harmless details (pet’s name, mother’s maiden name, your birthday) are often answers to security questions or could be used to impersonate you. For instance: posting “Happy 10th birthday to my son Jason!” gives away your son’s name and age – not strictly dangerous, but those building blocks can feed an identity puzzle. Or announcing “Can’t wait for my trip to Bali next week!” publicly – now people know you’ll be away (both online risks and offline). We’re not telling people to be paranoid, just to be selective. For novices, a rule of thumb: only share things you’d be okay with a stranger knowing, because once it’s online, it can spread beyond your control. Also caution against oversharing personal drama or financial info on social media (some people post stuff like “I just got my new credit card!” with a photo – obviously risky).
    • Managing Friend/Follower Requests: Advise users to be wary of accepting friend requests or direct messages from people they don’t actually know. Scammers often create fake profiles to befriend targets and gain trust. This especially affects older users sometimes (romance scams, etc.), but anyone can get a random “I saw you in this group, let’s connect” which then leads to a scam pitch. So, “if you don’t recognize the person, it’s okay to ignore or decline requests.” For existing contacts, if a friend’s account is acting weird (like sending you random links – a sign it might be hacked), reach out to them out-of-band before trusting any unusual messages.
    • Phishing on Social Media: Reinforce that phishing can happen on these platforms too. E.g., you might get a Facebook Messenger text like “OMG is this you in this video? \[link]” – which is a known scam that steals your login. So the earlier phishing lessons apply to links you receive on social apps as well. Also mention email or messages that claim to be from a social platform itself (like “Your account will be suspended, click here to verify”) – those are phishing attempts; instead, go to the official app/website to check your account.
    • Protecting Personal Data: We encourage users to guard sensitive info such as their Social Security Number (for US, or national ID in other countries), financial account numbers, etc. The average person should never share these via email or social messaging. If they need to provide such info (say for a job or official purpose), it should be through secure official channels. We will point out that identity thieves can piece together information from various sources – maybe your address from a public post, your email from another, etc. However, we will do this in an educational tone, not fearmongering.
    • Basic Data Privacy Practices: Beyond social media, just general privacy tips like: use messaging apps that offer encryption (for personal chats, apps like WhatsApp, iMessage are encrypted by default, which is good – we can briefly explain that encryption means only you and the recipient can read the messages). Also, when signing up for online services, be mindful of what info you provide and review privacy policies if something seems fishy. (We know hardly anyone reads privacy policies, but as Norton’s guide suggests, if a site won’t tell you how they use your data, that’s a red flag10.) We can simplify it to: stick to reputable sites for sensitive activities.
    • Opting Out and Permissions: Inform users about options like opting out of unnecessary data collection. For example, your mobile apps often ask for permissions – a flashlight app shouldn’t need to see your contacts or location. So approve permissions wisely; you can say “No” if something doesn’t make sense. Also, web browsers have settings or extensions for privacy (like tracking protection). These might be advanced for a newbie, so perhaps optional content.
Outcome: After this course, users will ideally tighten up their privacy settings and be more mindful of their digital footprint. The app could include an interactive element where it prompts: “Open your Facebook privacy settings now, and see if your birthday is public. If yes, consider restricting it.” Then user clicks a confirmation “I updated it” to get a completion checkmark. Similar for other big platforms. We might not be able to integrate with those apps directly, but we can provide a guided checklist that they do manually. This gives tangible actions to take. Also, they’ll understand why their personal information is precious. We can cite, for example, “In 2024, over 6 million identity theft and fraud reports were filed, often stemming from personal info falling into the wrong hands2.” By being cautious about sharing data, they reduce the likelihood of becoming part of those statistics.
Additionally, users gain social media literacy: knowing not everything or everyone online is trustworthy. This contributes to a safer overall posture – they become less likely to be tricked via social channels and less likely to leak info that enables other attacks.
5. Secure Finances & Identity Protection
Why it’s important: Financial accounts (banking, credit cards, online payments like PayPal) are prime targets for criminals because that’s where the money is. Everyday people might not realize how at-risk their finances are, or they might have misconceptions like “If something happens, the bank will just refund me.” While banks do have fraud protection, it’s far better to prevent fraud in the first place. Also, identity theft can go beyond stealing existing funds – criminals might take loans or open new credit lines in the victim’s name, causing long-term damage. Given that credit card fraud was 43.9% of identity theft cases in 20242, this is clearly a widespread problem. This course focuses on safeguarding the user’s financial well-being online and steps to take if something goes wrong.
Course content: Key components of this module:
    • Secure Online Banking Practices: Coach users on how to safely access their bank or other financial accounts. First, stress the importance of strong, unique passwords and 2FA on these accounts (which should already be covered in the Password module – here we reinforce, perhaps saying “If you do one thing, make sure your bank account has a unique password not used anywhere else, and enable SMS or app-based 2FA that your bank likely offers”). Next, advise to use official bank apps or bookmarks: e.g., use your bank’s mobile app or type the bank URL directly; do not click on banking links from emails/messages since phishing is rampant. If the bank sends an email about an issue, it’s safer to open the banking app yourself to check. Also, look for the security indicators (HTTPS padlock) when doing financial transactions online.
    • Monitoring and Alerts: Encourage enabling account alerts. Most banks allow you to get a text or email if a large transaction occurs, or if login from a new device happens. Such alerts can quickly catch unauthorized activity. The course suggests users turn these on (and maybe shows where, generically, or just encourages contacting their bank to set it up). We also encourage regularly checking bank statements or the account online for any unfamiliar transactions – making it a habit can catch fraud early. Given that one-third of Americans experienced financial fraud in a year1, vigilance is justified.
    • Safe Online Shopping: Many everyday users shop online; we impart tips to do it safely: stick to reputable e-commerce sites (Amazon, known retailers, or if using smaller sites, check reviews and that they have proper security like HTTPS). Use secure payment methods – for example, credit cards often have fraud protection, whereas wiring money or sending gift card codes is a red flag (scammers love to ask for payment in gift cards or cryptocurrency because it’s hard to trace and recover). We will mention never to send payment to someone you only communicated with via email/chat without a guaranteed process (like avoid off-platform transactions on sites like eBay/Craigslist where the platform protections are bypassed). Also, advise not saving card details on too many sites – or use a payment service (PayPal, Apple Pay, Google Pay) so that merchants don’t get your actual card number. This limits exposure if the merchant gets breached. We might recall the “non-delivery scam” being common1 and tell them to be cautious with deals that seem too good on random websites.
    • Credit Reports and Identity Monitoring: For identity protection, educate users that they have credit reports (in countries with credit bureaus) and that they can check them to see if any new accounts or loans have been opened in their name. In the US, one can get free annual credit reports from Equifax, Experian, TransUnion – we might not dive deep but mention it as a resource. Also, in case of known identity theft, one can place a fraud alert or credit freeze – again perhaps a brief mention with a pointer to resources, since implementing that might be beyond the scope of an app tutorial and more of a “if it happens, here’s what to do.”
    • Common Financial Scams: We underline specific scams aimed at getting money: e.g., phoney IRS/tax agent calls demanding payment, romance scams (where someone builds an online relationship then asks for money), lottery scams (“You won money, just pay a processing fee”), and investment scams (get-rich-quick schemes, fake cryptocurrency or stock investment opportunities). According to FBI data, investment scams are actually extremely costly (in 2022, investment fraud cost victims $3.3B, more than any other type)1. While our app is not an investment advisor, we can caution: “Be skeptical of unsolicited investment offers, or anyone online who guarantees profits – many are scams that can drain your savings.” For beginners, the rule might be: if someone you don’t know asks you to send them money, or asks you to receive money on their behalf (could be money mule schemes), that’s a big red flag.
    • What to Do if Hacked or Defrauded: Finally, this course will outline an action plan if the user suspects their financial info is compromised. For example, if their bank account is hacked or card stolen: contact the bank immediately – banks have 24/7 hotlines for fraud; they can freeze the account, reverse charges, etc. If personal info (like SSN) is stolen, consider credit freezes and notify authorities (in the US, the FTC’s identity theft site). We’ll provide a short list: “Notify your financial institutions, change passwords, check your other accounts, and report to the relevant authorities (e.g., police or consumer protection agencies).” The idea is to reduce panic and give clear steps, since falling victim can be scary. We’ll also reassure that many people have gone through this and there are systems to help recover – the user isn’t alone or “ruined” if it happens, as long as they take action.
Outcome: Users finishing this module should feel more in control of their financial security. They’ll have concrete measures in place: likely 2FA on their bank, alerts set up, a habit of reviewing statements, and more caution when transacting online. We might include a small table or list in the app summarizing do’s and don’ts for financial safety as a handy reference (e.g. Do: use strong passwords and 2FA, monitor accounts; Don’t: click bank links from emails, send money to strangers, etc.). By highlighting how prevalent financial cybercrime is (recalling stats like millions of reports and billions lost), we justify the effort. But by completing the course, the user can take pride in significantly lowering their risk of joining those statistics.
For instance, using a password manager and 2FA on a bank account alone greatly reduces the chance of an attacker breaking in via brute force or reused credentials. Being scam-aware means they won’t easily fall for con artists, whether it’s a fake refund call or a romance scam. If they do encounter a breach (say a store they shop at gets hacked and their card is stolen), they’ll likely catch unauthorized charges early thanks to alerts, and know how to respond. They will also understand the value of keeping personal data (like SSN, DOB) guarded to prevent identity theft. Overall, this course arms them with knowledge to keep their finances and identity safe, which is arguably the most personal and important area of security for an individual.

Beginner-Friendly Delivery: Design and Approach
Identifying the right content is only half the battle – how we deliver that content will determine whether users actually learn and take action. CyberPup must be approachable and even enjoyable for people with zero technical background. To achieve this, we will adopt a microlearning, gamified approach – breaking down the journey into small, achievable steps with plenty of positive reinforcement, just as the user requested. Below are the design principles and features that will make the CyberPup experience effective and engaging:
    • Bite-Sized Lessons: Each topic is divided into short modules that can be completed in a few minutes. Research shows that microlearning (short, focused training) boosts engagement by about 50% and significantly improves retention11 compared to long lectures. Users won’t be overwhelmed with walls of text or hour-long videos. For example, rather than a 30-minute seminar on “all about passwords,” we’ll have a 5-minute interactive tutorial on creating a strong password, then another 5 minutes on using a password manager, etc. This aligns with how Khan Academy teaches via quick videos and practice problems. The app’s interface will clearly indicate each mini-lesson and its goal (e.g., “Lesson 1: Update your phone’s OS” estimated 3 minutes). This lets users progress at their own pace, one small goal at a time.
    • Interactive and Hands-On: Wherever possible, the content will be interactive rather than passive. This could include quizzes, drag-and-drop exercises (for instance, sorting examples into “phishing” vs “legit” categories), and checklists of tasks to complete. Interactive elements keep users engaged and reinforce learning by doing. For cybersecurity, scenario-based questions work well (“What would you do if...”). By practicing in-app, users gain confidence for real-life situations. We will also encourage actual hands-on action: for instance, a lesson might instruct the user to open their email settings and enable 2FA, or to update a setting on their device, then come back and tick a box saying “I did it.” This way the app isn’t just theory – it’s driving real improvements on the fly. Notably, gamified challenges and quizzes can make learning feel like a game, which boosts motivation (one study found 73% of people felt gamification helped them learn faster12).
    • Gamification and Positive Feedback: The app will employ gamification techniques to make the experience fun and rewarding. This can include points, badges, progress bars, and celebratory messages. For example, completing a module might earn the user a badge like “Phishing Protector” or add points to their “Cyber Health score.” Each small accomplishment triggers positive feedback: maybe a friendly animation (like a happy CyberPup mascot wagging its tail) and a message like “Great job! You just made your account safer.” Research strongly supports this approach: 90% of users are more likely to continue a program that incorporates gamification (because it increases engagement and satisfaction)12. We will leverage that by rewarding every milestone. The courses can be structured like levels in a game – finish all lessons in “Password Security” to reach 100% in that category and unlock perhaps a summary quiz or even a simple certificate. We must ensure the feedback is encouraging, not patronizing. Phrases like “You’re doing great” and “Only 2 steps left to complete this section!” can motivate users to keep going.
    • Frequent Checkpoints and Summaries: To reinforce knowledge, we include frequent checkpoint quizzes and summary screens. After a few lessons, a quick 3-question quiz helps recap (“Check your understanding: which of these passwords is strongest?”, etc.). Immediate feedback will correct any misunderstandings. Summaries at the end of each category will list the key takeaways in plain language (almost like a checklist they can refer back to). These also serve as positive reinforcement – e.g., “In this chapter, you’ve: enabled updates on your devices, installed antivirus, and learned to avoid unsafe Wi-Fi. Terrific progress!” Reiterating accomplishments helps cement the user’s confidence and memory. According to learning science, revisiting key points improves retention, especially for beginners.
    • Plain Language, Minimal Jargon: All content will be written in clear, simple language with non-technical analogies to explain concepts. We will avoid acronyms and technical terms unless absolutely necessary – and if we use them, we’ll define them in context (e.g., “malware (malicious software)”). The tone will be conversational, like a helpful friend walking the user through steps, rather than an academic lecture. Any necessary technical terms (like “firewall” or “encryption”) will be explained with real-world analogies (a firewall as a security guard, encryption as secret code, etc.), as we have done in the outline. The reading level will be kept intentionally accessible. The goal is not to make the user a cybersecurity expert with a lot of jargon; it’s to give them practical know-how in language they use every day. For example, instead of saying “Use multi-factor authentication to mitigate credential compromise,” we’ll say “Add a second step to your login (a code or tap on your phone) so even if someone guesses your password, they still can’t get in.” This way, even those entirely new to tech can grasp the advice. We can gather feedback through beta testing with non-tech family members to ensure instructions are understandable.
    • Attractive and Clean UI: The user interface of CyberPup will be clean, friendly, and not cluttered with dense text. Key information might be presented with icons or illustrations (for example, a shield icon for security settings, an envelope icon for email). A visually clean design with plenty of white space, clear headers, and perhaps a gentle color scheme will make the app inviting. Each course category can have its own icon (as in the insights cards above: a key for passwords, shield for devices, etc.), reinforcing the theme visually. The design should avoid anything intimidating – so no excessive red warning signs or skull-and-crossbones imagery that could scare the user; instead, focus on positive symbols (green checkmarks, badges, our dog mascot giving a thumbs-up paw). User experience (UX) research in security apps suggests that empowering, user-centric design improves adoption. We want users to feel safe and guided, not punished. For example, if the user hasn’t done a task, we nudge them gently (“This step is important to protect you – give it a try!”) rather than showing a scary alert (“Your security is LOW!” which could cause anxiety). A logical navigation structure will allow users to easily find topics; maybe a dashboard shows all categories and their completion status, and the user can jump back in where they left off. Consistency in UI elements (buttons, back navigation, help tips) will ensure even novices can use the app without confusion.
    • Regular Progress Updates: A progress bar or dashboard will show the user how far they’ve come in the overall health check. This ties into gamification – seeing a progress bar go from 20% to 50% to 100% can be very motivating. We might break the journey into stages (e.g., Bronze, Silver, Gold levels of security, or simply “Step 3 of 10 completed”). This way, users always know there’s a finite set of steps – it’s not an endless open-ended process. Achieving 100% (or completion of all recommended steps) will be a moment of celebration in the app. Perhaps at the end, they receive a “Cyber Safe Certificate” or a summary of all the improvements they’ve made, reinforcing that they are now much safer than when they began.
    • Personalization and Platform-Specific Guidance: Being product-agnostic doesn’t mean being vague. We will present options tailored to the user’s context. The app could ask upfront or detect (with permission) what platform the user is on (e.g., iOS vs Android, Windows vs Mac). Then, within lessons, it can show the relevant instructions. For instance, in the password manager section, if the user indicated they have an iPhone, we’ll show how to use iCloud Keychain; if Android, we show Google Password Manager or Bitwarden setup. Similarly, for device security, the instructions for checking updates will differ for Windows PC vs. a Mac – we can branch or let the user select which guide to view. This keeps content relevant and avoids confusion of showing steps that don’t apply to the user’s device. It also reinforces product agnosticism: we’re not forcing a single solution, we’re offering choices suited to the user’s ecosystem. Another aspect of personalization: if the user has little knowledge, we might start very basic; if the user is a bit more savvy, maybe allow them to skip certain basics. However, since the target is zero understanding, we’ll assume minimal knowledge and not skip too much.
    • Non-Alarming Tone: Safety is important, but we’ll avoid fear-based messaging. The last thing we want is to terrify an everyday user that the internet is a booby-trapped minefield – that could lead to despair or avoidance. Instead, the tone is empowering: “Yes, there are bad guys out there, but you have the power to stop them from hurting you by taking these simple steps.” For example, rather than saying “If you don’t do X, you might lose all your money,” which is scary, we say “Doing X greatly reduces the chances a criminal could ever get to your money.” It’s a flip to the positive action. Where we present statistics about threats (like we did above: phishing frequency, etc.), it will be in context of “this is why we recommend these measures,” not to overwhelm users with doom. The idea is to create cyber awareness without cyber anxiety. By the end, users should feel in control of their security, not scared of it.
    • Reference and Support Materials: We will include easy-to-access help sections or “FAQ” for each category where users can read more if they want (for instance, if someone wants to know more about VPNs or about how a particular scam works in depth, they can find a short explainer). But these will be optional, possibly in a lighter font or a separate section called “Learn More (optional).” The main course stays focused on must-do actions. We’ll also provide resource links (to things like official how-to guides on enabling 2FA on various services, or consumer protection websites for reporting issues in their region). Product agnostic approach means we might list multiple resources – e.g., “Here are three password managers you could consider – \[link to external reviews]” – letting the user choose.
    • Feedback Loop: Finally, we can include a feedback mechanism for users to ask questions or indicate if something was unclear. Since it’s an app, maybe a chatbot or Q\&A forum might be ambitious, but even a feature like “Did you find this lesson helpful? Yes/No” and if No, ask what was confusing – could help us continuously improve the content. This ensures the content truly remains beginner-friendly because we adapt based on real user confusion points.
Why this approach works: This combination of microlearning and gamification is proven in educational research to improve learner outcomes. It keeps users from feeling overwhelmed by breaking the journey into digestible chunks, and it keeps them coming back through rewards and a sense of accomplishment. A meta-analysis in 2024 found gamification in education significantly increases student motivation and engagement12. And a Gartner study noted that a majority of learners prefer training in shorter, focused sessions11. By designing CyberPup this way, we align with those best practices. The app will meet users at their level and gradually raise their cyber savvy, all while making the process enjoyable.

Ensuring User Confidence and Empowerment
A central success criterion for CyberPup is that users finish the program feeling confident, not intimidated. The ultimate deliverable is not just a checklist of completed tasks, but a change in the user’s mindset – from “I don’t understand tech, I’m probably not safe” to “I can handle this; I know how to stay safe online now.” We will cultivate that confidence throughout the experience.
Several elements contribute to this empowerment:
    • Trackable Progress and Visible Results: As users proceed, they’ll see their progress visually (like leveling up). Upon completing all courses, present them with a summary of what they’ve achieved: e.g., “You secured 5 major areas of your digital life!” followed by bullet points: “✅ All your important accounts now have strong, unique passwords and 2-factor authentication. ✅ You can identify phishing attempts and avoid scams. ✅ Your devices are updated and protected against malware. ✅ Your social media privacy is tightened. ✅ Your financial accounts have extra safeguards.” Seeing this list reinforces that they’ve significantly improved their security posture. It’s essentially a before/after comparison – from having multiple vulnerabilities to now having robust protections in place. This tangible sense of improvement is crucial for confidence. We might even quantify their improvement in a playful way, like giving a “Cyber Health Score” at start vs end. For instance, an initial self-assessment quiz might yield a baseline score (many wrong answers or risky behaviors), and after training, a final quiz shows a dramatically higher score. That contrast can make them proud of their new knowledge.
    • Positive Reinforcement vs Fear: As mentioned, we use positive messaging. We will acknowledge that no one is 100% safe (even experts can slip up), but emphasize that following these practices puts the odds in their favor. The tone is “you have taken control of your security” rather than “threats are everywhere.” This sense of control combats the helplessness that non-tech users often feel. In safety communications, it’s known that fear appeals must be coupled with efficacy – we have to show them that the steps they took effectively reduce the danger, which we have with our cited stats (like “Using 2FA can block 99% of automated attacks” – a stat from Google we could include). By repeatedly showing that their actions matter, we make them feel empowered.
    • Continued Learning and Maintenance: We encourage a mindset of ongoing alertness without paranoia. For example, at the end we might say: “Stay vigilant, but remember you’ve learned the key skills now. Just keep practicing these good habits.” Perhaps suggest periodic refreshers or a yearly checkup. We can even build a feature to remind users every few months to review any new security news or re-check if new devices are properly secured. The idea is to integrate security into their routine. However, we must assure them that they don’t need to constantly worry – just to remain mindful. After completing CyberPup, everyday tasks like checking an email or installing an app will naturally trigger their new learned instincts (like “hmm, double-check this link” or “is my phone updated?”) and that’s a victory in itself.
    • Agnostic Recommendations = User Trust: By being product-agnostic and not pushing specific brands, users will sense that the app’s advice is in their best interest, not driven by sales. This builds trust in the content. For instance, when we say “use a password manager that suits you – here are a few options,” the user feels in control of the choice and doesn’t feel like they’re being advertised to. This trust contributes to their confidence in the knowledge imparted. They know it’s unbiased, coming from best practices endorsed by experts and authorities (we back up advice with references and logical reasons in the content, as we will behind the scenes).
    • Catering to Different Skill Levels Gently: Some users might pick things up quickly, others might struggle with certain tasks (like enabling a firewall could be confusing if they have an unusual setup). The app will provide help and not judge. If a user is stuck, perhaps a “Need help?” button offers extra guidance or connects them with a support resource (could be an FAQ or ideally a community forum moderated by experts). Ensuring no user is left behind or feeling dumb is key to them finishing the program confidently. The language will always blame the bad actors for any trouble, not the user. For example, instead of saying “You failed to update, which caused the virus,” we say “Cybercriminals took advantage of an outdated app on your device – now you know how to prevent that by updating.” The difference is subtle but important for user psychology: we emphasize that anyone can be a victim, but now the user has tools to fight back.
    • Real-World Validation: Where appropriate, we might include brief anecdotes or statistics to validate that the steps they took are proven. For instance, after the phishing course, noting something like “Organizations that train their people like this see far fewer successful phishing attacks4” or “People who use password managers have much less risk of account breaches” (if we find a citation) – this lets the user know these aren’t just hypothetical tips, they work. Seeing the link between what they did and how it prevents bad outcomes reinforces the value of their effort. Essentially: “By doing X, you’ve avoided Y” kind of statements.
    • Completion Reward: Finishing the entire health check could earn the user a special badge or even a certificate of completion (perhaps something they could print or share – though sharing might be ironic for a privacy app, but some might want to show off that they are “CyberPup Certified” safe users!). Even if just a graphic inside the app, it serves as a trophy. More importantly, the end of the journey should have a message congratulating the user for investing in their digital well-being. We could liken it to completing a health check at the doctor and getting a clean bill of health. Encourage them to celebrate and maybe tell family/friends that they’ve done this – maybe even refer others to use the app if that’s in scope (word-of-mouth can help spread security awareness).
    • Encouragement to Keep Up-to-Date: While the course covers current common threats, the cyber world evolves. We will quietly encourage users to keep learning whenever something new comes up (perhaps the app can push notifications about important security news written in simple terms, like “Major data breach at XYZ site – here’s what to do” as continuing education, though that’s a future feature). Knowing that they should keep an eye out for news (from reputable sources, not rumors) will help them maintain confidence that they can handle new challenges too. Basically, we’ve not just given fish, but taught them to fish: they’ve built a foundational understanding so that even if a new scam arises, they can apply similar principles to recognize and avoid it. That self-efficacy is the ultimate confidence booster.
In summary, by structuring CyberPup’s content into practical categories and delivering it with an engaging, supportive approach, we will enable regular people to significantly improve their cybersecurity without feeling lost or frightened. The combination of reputable information (weaving in impactful statistics and facts to highlight why each step matters) and beginner-friendly presentation (micro lessons, analogies, interactivity, and positive feedback) meets the goals set out: covering all major risks in a structured, accessible way, product-agnostic, and with user psychology in mind at every step.
CyberPup will function as a personal guide – much like a patient, loyal pup that guides someone across busy streets safely – helping users navigate the perils of the online world confidently. By the end of the program, users will not only have completed a thorough “cyber health check” of their devices and accounts, but they will also have gained lasting knowledge and habits. They’ll know that although cyber threats are real, they have the tools and know-how to fend them off, keeping themselves and their loved ones safe in the digital age. 吧
